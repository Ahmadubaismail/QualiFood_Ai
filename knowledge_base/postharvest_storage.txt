Postharvest Storage – Detailed Notes

Introduction

Postharvest storage refers to the safe handling and preservation of agricultural products after harvesting until they are consumed, processed, or marketed. The primary objective of postharvest storage is to maintain the quality, safety, nutritional value, and marketability of crops while minimizing quantitative and qualitative losses. Proper storage is especially important for grains, legumes, fruits, vegetables, and nuts because these commodities remain biologically active after harvest and can deteriorate if not stored under suitable conditions.

In developing countries, significant postharvest losses occur due to poor storage facilities, inadequate drying, pest infestation, microbial contamination, and poor handling practices. Effective postharvest storage helps improve food security, increases farmers' income, reduces waste, and ensures a stable food supply throughout the year.


---

Objectives of Postharvest Storage

The main objectives of postharvest storage are to:

Preserve the quality of agricultural products.

Prevent quantitative and qualitative losses.

Maintain nutritional value.

Extend shelf life.

Protect products from pests and diseases.

Ensure continuous food supply throughout the year.

Increase market value and profitability.

Maintain seed viability for future planting.



---

Importance of Postharvest Storage

Proper postharvest storage is important because it:

Reduces food losses.

Prevents spoilage.

Maintains food safety.

Preserves appearance, flavor, and texture.

Reduces contamination by microorganisms.

Prevents insect and rodent infestation.

Improves food availability during off-seasons.

Supports food security and economic development.



---

Factors Affecting Postharvest Storage

Several factors influence the success of postharvest storage.

1. Moisture Content

Moisture is one of the most important factors affecting storage quality. High moisture encourages fungal growth, insect infestation, and spoilage.

Recommended moisture levels include:

Maize: about 13–14%

Groundnuts: about 7–9%

Rice: about 12–14%


Proper drying before storage is essential.


---

2. Temperature

Temperature affects respiration, microbial growth, and insect activity.

High temperatures increase deterioration.

Low temperatures slow respiration and microbial growth.

Stable temperatures are preferred for long-term storage.



---

3. Relative Humidity

High humidity promotes mold growth and moisture absorption by stored products.

Low humidity helps maintain product quality and reduces spoilage.


---

4. Storage Duration

The longer products remain in storage, the greater the risk of deterioration if storage conditions are poor.


---

5. Pest Infestation

Insects, rodents, birds, and mites damage stored products by feeding on them and contaminating them with droppings and urine.

Common storage insects include:

Maize weevil (Sitophilus zeamais)

Rice weevil (Sitophilus oryzae)

Lesser grain borer (Rhyzopertha dominica)

Red flour beetle (Tribolium castaneum)



---

6. Microbial Growth

Bacteria, yeasts, and molds cause spoilage during storage.

Common storage molds include:

Aspergillus flavus

Aspergillus parasiticus

Penicillium species

Fusarium species


Some molds produce dangerous mycotoxins such as aflatoxins.


---

Types of Storage Systems

1. Traditional Storage

Examples include:

Mud silos

Clay pots

Woven baskets

Cribs

Underground pits


Advantages:

Low cost

Locally available materials


Disadvantages:

Poor protection against moisture and pests

Short storage life



---

2. Improved Storage

Examples include:

Metal silos

Hermetic bags (e.g., PICS bags)

Plastic silos

Warehouses

Cold storage facilities


Advantages:

Better protection against insects and moisture

Reduced postharvest losses

Improved product quality



---

Postharvest Handling Practices

Good postharvest handling includes:

Harvesting at the correct maturity stage.

Gentle handling to prevent mechanical damage.

Cleaning to remove dirt and foreign materials.

Sorting to remove damaged or diseased produce.

Proper drying to safe moisture levels.

Safe packaging.

Appropriate transportation.

Correct storage conditions.



---

Storage Pests and Their Control

Insects

Control methods include:

Hermetic storage

Regular inspection

Fumigation by trained personnel where appropriate

Sanitation

Proper drying


Rodents

Control methods include:

Sealing storage buildings

Traps

Good sanitation

Proper waste disposal


Birds

Control methods include:

Bird-proof storage facilities

Nets

Closed warehouses



---

Postharvest Diseases

Common diseases include:

Aspergillus rot

Penicillium rot

Fusarium rot

Rhizopus rot


Prevention includes:

Proper drying

Good hygiene

Careful handling

Temperature and humidity control

Removal of infected products



---

Storage of Grains and Groundnuts

Grains should be:

Cleaned before storage.

Dried to safe moisture content.

Stored in clean, dry containers.

Protected from insects and rodents.

Inspected regularly.


Groundnuts should:

Be dried immediately after harvest.

Be stored in well-ventilated facilities.

Be protected from moisture.

Be sorted to remove moldy kernels.



---

Quality Changes During Storage

During storage, products may experience:

Moisture gain or loss

Loss of nutrients

Color changes

Flavor deterioration

Weight loss

Insect damage

Mold growth

Mycotoxin contamination

Reduced seed viability



---

Good Storage Practices

Clean storage facilities before use.

Dry products properly before storage.

Use pallets to keep bags off the floor.

Keep stores well ventilated.

Monitor temperature and humidity.

Practice First-In, First-Out (FIFO).

Inspect products regularly.

Remove spoiled products immediately.

Maintain good sanitation.

Keep accurate storage records.



---

Benefits of Proper Postharvest Storage

Reduced food losses.

Improved food quality.

Increased shelf life.

Better food safety.

Higher market prices.

Reduced pest infestation.

Reduced mold growth.

Lower risk of mycotoxin contamination.

Improved household and national food security.



---

Challenges of Postharvest Storage

Poor storage infrastructure.

High humidity in tropical climates.

Lack of drying equipment.

Pest infestations.

Inadequate farmer training.

Limited access to improved storage technologies.

Poor transportation systems.

High cost of modern storage facilities.



---

Recommendations

Train farmers in proper postharvest handling.

Promote the use of improved storage technologies such as hermetic bags and metal silos.

Encourage proper drying before storage.

Monitor moisture content regularly.

Improve warehouse sanitation.

Strengthen pest management programs.

Increase awareness of food safety and mycotoxin prevention.



---

Conclusion

Postharvest storage is a critical component of the food supply chain because it preserves the quality, safety, and nutritional value of agricultural products after harvest. Proper drying, careful handling, effective pest control, and suitable storage conditions significantly reduce postharvest losses and help prevent contamination by molds and mycotoxins such as aflatoxins. Adopting good storage practices and modern storage technologies enhances food security, improves farmers' incomes, and ensures that consumers receive safe, high-quality food.
